1. Overview
The MVP does not aim to:
	•	replace Nostr  
	•	replace NIP-53 clients  
	•	build a new social graph  
	•	solve global moderation  
	•	persist reputation or identity  
If any of those appear, the MVP has failed.



2. Roles 
Broadcaster
AC-2.1 Broadcaster is defined as:
	•	the initiator of the NIP-53 live event  
	•	the issuer of session configuration
AC-2.2 Broadcaster has no inherent moderation power beyond:
	•	appointing/removing moderators  
	•	setting tolerance thresholds  
	•	choosing whether Rolodex participation is enabled
Broadcaster is not required to:
	•	moderate chat  
	•	perform socially  
	•	be present continuously
Attendees
AC-2.3 Attendees:
	•	join a session via a valid session key/token  
	•	may participate without revealing persistent identity beyond Nostr pubkey  
	•	may leave and re-join without penalty (subject to gating)
AC-2.4 Attendees have no discoverability outside the session.
👉 Test: An attendee cannot be found, followed, or referenced once the session ends.
Moderators
AC-2.5 Moderators are:
	•	attendees granted temporary ban permission  
	•	appointed and removed at broadcaster discretion  
	•	indistinguishable from attendees outside of permission scope
AC-2.6 Moderator status:
	•	expires automatically when session ends  
	•	does not persist across sessions  
	•	is not globally visible



3. Session lifecycle 
AC-1.1 A Skyless session MUST have:
	•	a clear start  
	•	a clear end  
	•	a finite TTL
AC-1.2 When a session ends:
	1	Session-level moderation state (user count, user lists, bans, bpm counters, tolerance thresholds) MUST be destroyed.

	2	Moderator aggregate metrics MAY persist, but MUST be:
	◦	quantitative only  
	◦	non-discoverable by users  
	◦	disconnected from discovery, ranking, or reach  
AC-1.3 Re-starting a session with the same broadcaster does not inherit:
	•	moderators  
	•	tolerance configuration (unless explicitly re-set)
👉 Test: Kill the client, restart the same stream → governance resets.



4. Access control 
AC-3.1 Joining a Skyless session requires a cryptographic session key or token.
AC-3.2 Session keys MAY be issued via:
	•	time gating  
	•	payment  
	•	invite links  
	•	broadcaster-controlled issuance
AC-3.3 Session keys:
	•	are valid only for the session TTL  
	•	cannot be reused in future sessions  
	•	do not encode identity or reputation
👉 Test: A key from yesterday cannot join today’s session.



4. Rolodex 
AC-4.1 Users may be randomly paired with other users during a session.
AC-4.2 Pairing:
	•	is opt-in for users  
	•	may be enabled or disabled by broadcaster  
	•	is ephemeral (connections dissolve automatically)
AC-4.3 Rolodex connections:
	•	do not persist  
	•	do not create friend/follow relationships  
	•	do not affect discoverability
👉 Test: Two attendees who connect cannot find each other after session end unless they exchange info manually.



5. Moderation + BPM 
AC-5.1 Moderators can ban users within the session.
AC-5.2 Each ban increments a bans-per-minute (bpm) counter:
	•	per session  
	•	per moderator (session-local)
AC-5.3 BPM values:
	•	are visible during the session  
	•	decay over time (rolling window)  
	•	reset to zero when the session ends
AC-5.4 Broadcaster can set a tolerance threshold:
	•	high bpm tolerated → strict environment  
	•	low bpm tolerated → permissive environment
AC-5.5 No BPM data is:
	•	stored across sessions  
	•	queryable globally  
	•	exportable as reputation
👉 Test: A “strict” moderator in one session has no carryover effect in the next.
1. Raw session state (DESTROYED)
This is anything that would let you replay, audit, or narrativize a session.
Destroyed at session end:
	•	individual ban events (who banned whom)  
	•	timestamps tied to a specific session  
	•	participant identities  
	•	Rolodex pairings  
	•	transcripts, chat logs  
	•	per-session BPM counters  
	•	per-session tolerance configuration
Once destroyed:
	•	you cannot say what happened  
	•	you cannot say to whom  
	•	you cannot say why
This is what preserves ephemerality.
2. Derived moderator aggregates (RETAINED)
These are lossy summaries, deliberately designed so they cannot be reverse-engineered into events.
Think of them as instrument calibration, not history.
Examples of what persists per moderator:
	•	total_bans_lifetime  
	•	total_active_minutes_lifetime  
	•	mean_bans_per_minute (rolling or EMA)  
	•	ban_latency_distribution (e.g. p50 / p90 time-to-ban)  
	•	disconnect_escalation_rate (how often peer disconnects lead to mod action)
Crucially:
	•	no session IDs  
	•	no timestamps that map to sessions  
	•	no user identifiers  
	•	no content references  
	•	no causal chains
You cannot reconstruct a story from this.
You can only infer style.


7. Non-accumulation guarantees (explicit)
AC-7.1 Skyless MUST NOT provide:
	•	follower graphs  
	•	reputation scores  
	•	persistent moderation records  
	•	global trust metrics
AC-7.2 Any attempt to add these breaks MVP acceptance.
This is a hard stop.
8. Integration with NIP-53 (practical)
AC-8.1 MVP works with existing NIP-53 live events.
AC-8.2 Does not require custom relays.
AC-8.3 Can be implemented as:
	•	client extension  
	•	middleware layer  
	•	optional plugin

5. Governance Telemetry 
5.1 Purpose and Scope
Governance Telemetry provides limited, quantitative aggregates to support session-level governance selection without preserving session history, participant identity, or qualitative judgment.
This section specifies the only persistent, accumulative signals maintained by Skyless: Broadcaster Aggregate (X) and Moderator Aggregate (Y). All other session-coupled state (participants, connections, ban events, per-session counters, transcripts) is ephemeral and MUST NOT persist beyond the session TTL.
Skyless telemetry aggregates exist solely to enable session governance selection:
	•	X expresses a broadcaster’s environmental configuration tendencies.  
	•	Y expresses a moderator’s intervention style.
Aggregates MUST remain non-semantic (no “reasons”), non-attributable (no session IDs), and non-public (not used for discovery/ranking).
5.1.1 Definitions
	•	Session-coupled data: Any data that would allow reconstruction of “what happened” in a specific session (e.g., ban events, timestamps tied to session, participant identities, transcripts, per-session ban counts).  
	•	Derived aggregate: A lossy summary computed incrementally over time (e.g., totals, EMAs, streaming quantiles) that cannot be reliably reverse-engineered into session events.  
5.2 Normative Requirements
These are the constraints.
1. Persistent Boundaries  

	1	Implementations MUST destroy all session-coupled data at session end (TTL expiration), including:
	1	ban events (who banned whom, when),  
	2	per-session ban counters,  
	3	participant lists and identifiers,  
	4	Rolodex pairings,  
	5	tolerance configuration used in that specific session,  
	6	any stored samples enabling replay or audit.
2. Implementations MAY maintain derived aggregates (X/Y) provided that:
	7	no session identifiers are stored,  
	8	no user identifiers are stored,  
	9	no content, transcript, or reason codes are stored,  
	10	no timestamps that bind an action to a specific session are stored.
3. Visibility and non-accumulation constraints
	1	X and Y MUST NOT be used for global ranking, discovery sorting, or recommendation.  
	2	X and Y MUST NOT be publicly queryable without authorization.  
	3	X and Y SHOULD be visible only to:  
	1	the broadcaster (X, and Y for moderators they may appoint), and  
	2	a moderator viewing their own Y.  
	4	Implementations MUST NOT surface “good/bad/trusted/untrusted” labels derived from X/Y.  
4. Computation constraints

	1	Aggregates MUST be computed incrementally without retaining event logs. Implementations SHOULD use:  
	1	totals/counters,  
	2	exponential moving averages (EMA),  
	3	streaming quantile approximations (e.g., P², t-digest) for latency percentiles.  
	8	Aggregates MUST be lossy: they MUST NOT retain sufficient data to reconstruct per-session ban histories.  
5. Latency measurement
	1	If ban_latency_* is implemented, the “latency clock start” MUST be triggered by non-semantic system events only, such as:
	1	a peer-disconnect escalation threshold being crossed, or  
	2	a system-generated “review requested” event, or  
	3	a moderator-user pairing start (if supported).  
	2	Latency MUST NOT be computed from content inspection (no NLP, keyword triggers, or transcript analysis).



5.3 Broadcaster Aggregate (X)
Broadcaster Aggregate describes the broadcaster’s governance posture across time, without encoding popularity or audience size.
X.1 sessions_total (integer)
Number of sessions created by this broadcaster.
	•	Purpose: basic denominator
X.2 session_minutes_total (integer)
Total live minutes across all sessions.
	•	Purpose: denominator to avoid trivial inflation
X.3 tolerance_target (float or small int bucket)
The broadcaster’s configured tolerance setting for a session, aggregated as an EMA or modal bucket.
	•	Purpose: environment signature  
	•	IMPORTANT: this is what you meant by “low tolerance conservative / high tolerance liberal” (or invert it if you prefer)
X.4 moderator_bpm_selected_ema (float)
EMA of the median bpm_ema of moderators selected per session.
	•	Purpose: what kind of stewards they prefer  
	•	Keeps it simple: “who you choose” becomes legible as a scalar
X.5 moderator_count_median (integer)
Median number of moderators appointed per session.
	•	Purpose: indicates governance style (hands-on vs light-touch), without needing attendance count
X.6 gating_mode_distribution (enum counts)
Counts of sessions by gating mode:
	•	open  
	•	time_gated  
	•	pay_gated  
	•	invite_gated  
	•	Purpose: tells you if the venue tends to be open/closed without tracking people
Implementations MAY include X-opt fields, but MUST NOT include audience counts or any metric that functions as a prestige signal.
Minimal X set: X.2 + X.3 + X.4
(add X.6 if you want “venue policy” legibility)



5.4 Moderator Aggregate (Y)
Moderator Aggregate describes intervention style (frequency + latency) without storing reasons or user-level outcomes.
Y.1 active_minutes_total (integer)
Total minutes the moderator held moderation permissions while a session was live.
	•	Purpose: normalize everything else  
	•	Leakage risk: low (no session IDs)
Y.2 bans_total (integer)
Total bans executed while holding moderation permissions.
	•	Purpose: core intervention measure
Y.3 bpm_ema (float)
Exponential moving average of bans-per-minute over the moderator’s lifetime window.
	•	Purpose: stable “style” indicator  
	•	Must be computed from Y.1 and Y.2 without storing events
Y.4 ban_latency_p50_seconds (integer)
Median time from first moderator-observable “flag condition” to ban. (See note below.)
	•	Purpose: distinguishes “surface/obvious” vs “contextual” bans without semantics  
	•	This is your key qualitative proxy—still quantitative
Y.5 ban_latency_p90_seconds (integer)
90th percentile of that latency.
	•	Purpose: robustness; prevents p50-only gaming
Y.6 peer_disconnect_escalations_total (integer)
Count of times an attendee was escalated to moderator review after repeated peer disconnects (Rolodex) and the moderator took an action (ban or explicit dismiss).
	•	Purpose: measures how often moderation is invoked by social friction signals  
	•	Still not judging correctness
Implementations MUST NOT store any mapping from bans to users, sessions, or content.
Minimal Y set: Y.1–Y.4 (you can ship without Y.5–Y.6). The “full minimal safe” set is Y.1–Y.6.
Note: defining “flag condition” for latency
To keep this minimal and not semantic, define the start of the latency clock as one of:
	•	first peer-disconnect threshold trigger against the user (e.g., 3 disconnects within 60s), or  
	•	first report request generated by the system (not by text analysis), or  
	•	first time the user is paired with a moderator (if you implement mod-pairing)
No content inspection needed.
5.5 X/Y Alignment
Governance decisions MUST be based on the alignment of broadcaster aggregate (X) and moderator aggregate (Y).
Aggregates MUST NOT be interpreted independently as indicators of merit.




6. Security & Privacy
This section enumerates the intended protections, explicit non-goals, and residual risks of Skyless moderation telemetry (X/Y). Implementations MUST adhere to these constraints to preserve non-accumulative governance.
6.1 Threat Model
Skyless assumes an adversarial environment in which:
	•	participants may attempt to reconstruct session history,  
	•	moderators or broadcasters may attempt to game aggregates,  
	•	external observers may attempt to infer popularity, ideology, or legitimacy,  
	•	implementers may be tempted to extend aggregates into reputation systems.
The design prioritizes ephemerality, non-attribution, and pluralism over auditability or correctness guarantees.
6.2 Protections Provided
A. Session Reconstruction Prevention
Skyless prevents reconstruction of individual sessions by requiring that:
	1	No session identifiers are stored in aggregates.  
	2	No per-session counters or samples are retained.  
	3	No timestamps are stored that bind actions to a specific session window.  
	4	No participant identities are stored or linkable.
As a result, an observer with access to X/Y aggregates MUST NOT be able to determine:
	•	which users were banned,  
	•	when a ban occurred,  
	•	which session a ban occurred in,  
	•	why a ban occurred,  
	•	how many participants were present.
This property is intentional and MUST be preserved.
B. Reputation Drift Prevention
Skyless prevents moderation telemetry from becoming a reputation system by enforcing:
	1	Non-semantic metrics only   Aggregates encode frequency and latency, never correctness, intent, or moral judgment.  
	2	No global visibility   X/Y MUST NOT be surfaced in public discovery, ranking, or recommendation systems.  
	3	No automatic consequences   X/Y MUST NOT trigger automated sanctions, promotions, throttling, or boosts.  
	4	Interpretation externalized to broadcasters   The system does not label values as “good,” “bad,” “trusted,” or “abusive.”
Under these constraints, aggregates remain instrument calibration, not status.
C. Privacy Preservation
Skyless moderation telemetry is designed to be privacy-preserving by default:
	•	No content inspection is required or permitted.  
	•	No transcripts, messages, or media are retained.  
	•	No user-level records exist.  
	•	No appeals or case histories are stored.
This significantly reduces:
	•	data retention liability,  
	•	legal discovery exposure,  
	•	chilling effects on speech,  
	•	incentives for performative moderation.
6.3 Explicit Non-Goals
Skyless does not attempt to provide:
	•	correctness of moderation decisions,  
	•	fairness guarantees,  
	•	explainability of bans,  
	•	appeals processes,  
	•	global trust or abuse registries,  
	•	cross-session accountability.
These properties are intentionally excluded because they require persistent identity, semantic judgment, and narrative memory, which conflict with Skyless’s design goals.
6.4 Residual Risks and Mitigations
The following risks are acknowledged and accepted:
A. Moderator Gaming
Risk:
A moderator may intentionally delay bans to lower perceived bpm_ema, or ban aggressively to signal strictness.
Mitigation:
	•	Latency percentiles (p50/p90) reduce simple gaming.  
	•	Selection is broadcaster-specific; no universal incentive exists.  
	•	No automatic rewards are tied to aggregate values.
This is considered acceptable; style signaling is a feature, not a bug.
B. Broadcaster Collusion or Sybil Moderation
Risk:
A broadcaster may appoint colluding moderators or repeatedly self-moderate.
Mitigation:
	•	No public metrics exist to exploit.  
	•	Session size and attendance are not surfaced.  
	•	Benefits of collusion do not escape the session boundary.
Because there is no global leverage, such behavior yields minimal advantage.
C. Aggregate Inference Over Long Time Horizons
Risk:
Over very long durations, aggregates may become more descriptive of an actor’s style.
Mitigation (RECOMMENDED):
	•	Implementations SHOULD apply rolling windows or decay functions.  
	•	Implementations MAY allow broadcasters to voluntarily reset their own X.  
	•	Implementations MAY allow moderators to voluntarily reset their Y.
Reset capability MUST NOT be rate-limited or penalized.
6.5 Implementation Guidance (Non-Normative)
Implementers are encouraged to:
	•	Treat X/Y as calibration dials, not scores.  
	•	Design UIs that show ranges or bands rather than precise values.  
	•	Avoid longitudinal graphs that suggest “progress” or “improvement.”  
	•	Avoid comparative displays (no side-by-side ranking).
Any UI that induces competition, prestige, or shame violates the spirit of this specification.
6.6 Design Invariant (Normative)
Skyless MUST destroy narratives and preserve only signals necessary for situational governance.
Any extension that enables storytelling, judgment, or durable hierarchy MUST be considered a protocol violation.
